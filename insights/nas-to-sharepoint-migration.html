Migrating millions of files off an aging NAS to SharePoint — without breaking the creative pipeline.

For IQHQ, a life-sciences real-estate developer, the files weren't just documents — they were a living creative and engineering pipeline, where one InDesign layout pointed at dozens of linked images and a single CAD sheet referenced a stack of external blocks. Millions of files, decades of project history, and a hard standard for "done": zero broken links at cutover.

Field note · Microsoft 365 & Migrations · March 2026 · 12 min read

"Move the file server to SharePoint" reads like a copy job. It isn't. For IQHQ, a life-sciences real-estate developer, the files weren't just documents — they were a living creative and engineering pipeline, where one InDesign layout pointed at dozens of linked images and a single CAD sheet referenced a stack of external blocks. Break those links and you don't lose files. You lose work.

The brief sounded simple: an aging network-attached storage (NAS) appliance was running out of headroom, the offsite team couldn't reach it without a clumsy VPN, and leadership wanted everything in Microsoft 365 — SharePoint and OneDrive — for resilience, search, and remote access. Millions of files. Decades of accumulated project history.

What made it interesting wasn't the volume. It was what the files were to each other. A document management system for a design-and-development firm isn't a flat pile of PDFs. It's a graph of references — and a migration that ignores the graph will technically "succeed" while quietly detonating every linked asset on the other side.

Here's how we moved it, what nearly went wrong, and the lessons we'd hand the next team doing the same thing.

Why a file-server migration isn't a copy job

Three things separate a creative-pipeline migration from a routine lift-and-shift:

1. Linked assets travel in graphs, not files. Design tools like Adobe InDesign and Illustrator, and CAD applications, don't embed everything they use. They link to it. An InDesign document holds pointers to placed images; a CAD sheet references external blocks and drawings. Those pointers are resolved at open time against a path. Change where the asset lives, and the pointer dangles — the layout opens with "missing link" warnings, low-res previews, or nothing at all. Multiply that across a library where a single deliverable might pull from dozens of locations, and an unplanned move can break thousands of references in an afternoon.

2. SharePoint enforces limits a NAS never did. A file server is forgiving. It happily stores a 380-character path twelve folders deep with a "#" in a project name. SharePoint and OneDrive are not so relaxed — there are limits on total URL/path length, and historically a set of characters and patterns that misbehave. Folder structures that lived comfortably on the NAS for fifteen years can hit a hard wall the moment they're rehomed under a site and library, which add their own prefix to every path.

3. "Done" is defined by the people downstream. Success here isn't "the bytes arrived." It's "the design lead opened Monday's project and every link resolved." That standard changes how you plan, test, and cut over.

A migration that ignores the link graph will technically "succeed" while quietly detonating every linked asset on the other side.

The approach: map before you move

We treated the first phase as archaeology, not engineering. Before touching a single byte in anger, we built a complete picture of what we were dealing with.

Inventory and profile the source. We scanned the entire NAS to produce a real inventory — total files, total volume, file-type distribution, age and last-touched dates, the deepest folder nesting, and the longest paths. That inventory did two jobs: it sized the migration honestly, and it surfaced every path that was going to be a problem long before it became one.

Find the long paths early. The single most valuable artifact of the whole project was a ranked list of the longest paths in the library, scored against the destination's limits. Most were fine. A meaningful tail were not — and those were the time bombs. Finding them before go-live, when we could still negotiate fixes calmly, was the difference between a clean cutover and a week of firefighting.

Design the destination structure deliberately. We didn't mirror the NAS folder-for-folder. We designed a target structure in SharePoint that kept the team's mental model intact while shortening the paths that mattered — flattening over-nested trees, retiring dead archives, and choosing site and library names that didn't eat the path budget. The goal: a place that felt familiar to open, but fit inside the platform's rules.

The principle we kept returning to. Every character spent on a long site name, library name, or deeply nested folder is a character stolen from the actual filename. On a NAS that math never mattered. On SharePoint it decides whether a file makes the trip. Budget the path like it's a scarce resource — because it is.

The gotchas — and how we defused each one

Gotcha #1: Path length, solved before cutover. This is the one that stops migrations cold, and it's the one we refused to discover at go-live. Armed with the ranked long-path report, we worked through the offenders methodically: flatten the structure here, shorten an archival folder name there, split an overstuffed tree into two. By the time we migrated for real, the path-length problem was already solved on paper — the move was just executing a plan we'd already validated.

Gotcha #2: Keeping linked references intact. The creative and engineering links were the whole game. Our rule was to preserve the relationships the tools rely on — to move related assets together and keep their relative arrangement consistent, so that when a layout or a drawing went looking for its pieces, the pieces were where it expected them to be. Where the platform changed how files are addressed, we made sure the team had a clear, low-friction way to reconnect — never a silent break they'd stumble onto mid-deadline.

We didn't want the design lead to discover a broken link on a deadline. We wanted there to be no broken link to discover.

Gotcha #3: Scale, throttling, and the moving target. Millions of files is a different sport than thousands. The cloud throttles aggressively under sustained load — and rightly so — so a migration has to be paced, retried intelligently, and run in waves rather than as one heroic overnight push. Just as important: the NAS doesn't hold still while you copy it. People keep working. We planned for the delta — the files that changed after the first pass — with a final reconciliation sync close to cutover, so the destination matched reality at the moment of switchover, not the moment we started.

Gotcha #4: Permissions that mean the same thing on both sides. NAS permissions and Microsoft 365 sharing are different models. A faithful migration isn't just moving files; it's making sure the right people still have exactly the right access — and, just as importantly, that the wrong people don't suddenly gain it. We mapped access intent deliberately rather than trusting a blind translation.

Cutover: a non-event, on purpose

The best compliment a migration can get is that nobody noticed it happened. We ran the bulk of the data ahead of time, did a tight final reconciliation of everything that had changed, switched the team to the new home, and validated the references the creative and engineering teams actually depended on — before they sat down to use them.

The outcome. 0 broken links at go-live · millions of files relocated to Microsoft 365 · the path-length problem solved before the move, not after.

The headline outcome: zero broken links at cutover. The creative pipeline kept running. The engineering references resolved. The offsite team finally got to their files without wrestling a VPN. And the platform the firm landed on gave them search, versioning, and resilience the NAS never could.

What we'd tell the next team

  • Map before you move. The inventory and the ranked long-path report aren't busywork — they are the project. Everything clean downstream traces back to knowing exactly what sat upstream.
  • Solve path length on paper, not at go-live. Surface the offenders early, when fixes are calm decisions instead of cutover-week emergencies.
  • Migrate the graph, not the files. Move related assets together so a layout finds its links where it expects them — and reconcile the delta against live reality before you switch anyone over.

If you're staring at an aging file server, a creative or engineering pipeline you can't afford to break, or a SharePoint move that stalled on path-length errors — we start with the inventory and the long-path report, design a destination that fits the platform's rules, and rehearse the cutover before it's real. The goal is the same every time: zero surprises for the people who open the files on Monday.
