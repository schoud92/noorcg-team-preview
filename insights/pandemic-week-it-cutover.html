March 2020 was the worst possible week to change your IT provider — which is exactly when Sabra Health Care REIT, a publicly traded healthcare REIT, moved its entire environment to us. Offices were emptying, the whole company was going remote overnight, and a public filer can't afford a single day where people can't work. We cut over anyway. And the company came out the other side more secure than it went in.

Provider transitions are stressful in calm times. You're taking responsibility for an environment you didn't build, under a clock, with a business that has to keep running every minute of the handover. Now layer on a global pandemic declared the same week, a workforce that just lost its offices, and the scrutiny that comes with being a public company — where downtime isn't just an inconvenience, it's a reportable problem.

This is the story of how that cutover stayed clean, and why "more secure" was the outcome rather than a lucky side effect. It's deliberately high-level — the specifics of any client's security posture stay private, always — but the playbook for executing under pressure travels well. Years later, Sabra is still our managed partner, and its leadership has been candid about what that week meant:

"We migrated our IT services to Noor Consulting Group in March 2020, right as the COVID-19 pandemic began. The NCG team executed the transition flawlessly, significantly enhancing our IT environment and cybersecurity posture."
Michael Costa — EVP, Finance & Chief Accounting Officer · Sabra Health Care

The setup: the worst week to switch, and no option to wait

A few things were true at once, and all of them pulled against each other:

  • The timing wasn't negotiable. The transition was already in motion when the world changed. Pausing wasn't safer — it would have left the company between providers during the exact week it most needed solid ground.
  • The office was disappearing. Whatever assumptions the old setup made about people being on a corporate network in a building were evaporating in real time. The new normal was remote, immediately.
  • Public-company stakes. A REIT that trades on a public market operates under a level of accountability that turns "a bit of downtime" into a real problem. Continuity wasn't a nice-to-have.

The unspoken brief was simple: take the keys, keep everyone working, and don't make the move the story.

What made the cutover clean

Clean cutovers aren't luck. They come from a handful of disciplines applied without shortcuts. None of these are glamorous — that's the point.

1. Understand what you're inheriting before you own it. You can't run an environment you haven't mapped. Before taking responsibility, we built an honest picture of what was actually there — what people depended on day to day, what was load-bearing, and where the quiet fragilities lived. The goal of that first pass isn't to fix everything; it's to make sure there are no surprises on the day you become responsible.

2. Prioritize "can people work?" above everything. With offices closing, the single question that mattered was whether a remote workforce could do their jobs on day one. So the sequence of the cutover was built around continuity first — make sure the things people touch every hour keep working, then move outward to the rest. A transition that protects the daily workflow buys you the goodwill and the calm to do the deeper work properly.

3. Communicate like the humans are the system. Under pressure, the technical plan is only half the job. People who can't reach a file or sign in don't care about the migration plan — they care about getting unstuck. A predictable way to get help, clear expectations about what's changing, and a fast feedback loop kept a stressful week from turning into a panicked one. The cutover succeeds in people's experience of it, not just on the infrastructure.

4. Sequence for reversibility. The safest way to move fast is to move in steps you can stand behind. Rather than one all-or-nothing flip, a transition that proceeds in deliberate stages — each validated before the next — means a problem is contained to a step, not a catastrophe across the whole environment. Pressure is the worst time to bet everything on a single irreversible moment.

The mindset that held it together. Under real pressure, the instinct is to rush and to batch. We did the opposite: tight sequencing, one validated step at a time, and continuity treated as the non-negotiable. Slower-looking discipline is what produces a fast-feeling result.

Why the result was "more secure"

Here's the part people find counter-intuitive: a rushed, pandemic-week handover ended with a stronger security posture than the company started with. Not because of any single dramatic fix, but because a provider transition is a rare moment when someone finally looks at the whole environment with fresh eyes and current expectations.

Inheriting an environment forces a fresh, end-to-end assessment — and that naturally surfaces the gaps that accumulate quietly in any setup that's been running for years. A workforce going fully remote also changes the threat model: the old assumptions baked into "everyone's in the office" stop holding. The foundation we built toward was Microsoft 365 and identity-centric by design — security anchored to who the user is and the context they're signing in from, rather than to a corporate network perimeter that no longer existed. Conditional Access became the policy layer that decides, in context, when a sign-in should be allowed, challenged, or blocked — exactly the control a newly remote public company needs. Stepping in at that inflection point meant the environment got rebuilt around how Sabra was actually going to work, not how it used to.

We'll keep the specifics of what changed private — that's a permanent commitment to every client. But the shape of it is generalizable: a clean transition and a security uplift are the same project when you treat "taking over an environment" as a reason to assess it properly rather than just keep the lights on.

What we'd tell the next team

  • Continuity is the brief. When a business has to keep running through a handover, "can people work today?" outranks everything else — sequence the whole cutover behind it.
  • Move in reversible steps. Pressure is exactly when to refuse the single irreversible flip; validated stages contain a problem instead of multiplying it.
  • Use the transition to get more secure. Inheriting an environment is the natural moment to rebuild it around how the business actually works now — a clean cutover and a security uplift can be one project.

If you're switching providers, inheriting an environment that's been neglected, or moving to a remote-first footing — we map what you've got before we own it, sequence the cutover around keeping people working, and use the transition as the moment to close the gaps that accumulated over the years. The aim is a handover nobody outside IT even notices, and a posture that's stronger on the other side.
