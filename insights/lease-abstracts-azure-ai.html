Field note

Reading lease abstracts with Azure AI — and a Claude assistant that actually knows the data.

For Sabra Health Care REIT, the facts buried in forty pages of dense lease prose drive rent rolls, renewal calendars, and the asset numbers that flow up to the financials. Two layers made them trustworthy: Azure AI document extraction for the first pass, and a data-aware Claude assistant that answers questions grounded in the actual rows — not the model's imagination.

Field note · Applied AI & Document Intelligence · April 2026 · 13 min read

A commercial lease is forty pages of dense, bespoke legal prose written to be argued over — not parsed. For a publicly-traded healthcare REIT, the facts buried in those pages drive rent rolls, renewal calendars, and the asset numbers that flow up to the financials. The job was never "read the PDF." It was: turn an unstructured lease into trustworthy, structured fields — and then let a human ask the resulting report questions in plain English without it making anything up.

This is the story of two layers we built for Sabra Health Care REIT, the healthcare real-estate operator we've run as managed partner since 2020. The first layer extracts a structured lease abstract from raw documents using Azure AI document intelligence. The second layer puts a data-aware Claude assistant on top of the finished lease report, so an analyst can interrogate the numbers in conversation — grounded in the actual rows, not the model's imagination.

Both layers were easy to demo and hard to make trustworthy. The distance between those two states is the whole post.

Layer one: extracting the abstract

A "lease abstract" is the structured summary a lease administration team lives by: the parties, the leased premises, the key dates (commencement, expiration, options), the rent schedule, the escalations, the renewal terms. Lawyers write the lease. Abstractors translate it into rows. That translation is slow, repetitive, and exactly the kind of work that quietly accumulates error when it's done by hand across hundreds of agreements.

We used Azure AI document intelligence to do the first read. The model ingests the lease, recognizes its layout, and pulls the fields we care about into a structured shape that lands in the lease report rather than a person's eyes and a spreadsheet. The point isn't to remove the human — it's to move the human from transcription to verification. Reading forty pages to find one renewal clause is drudgery. Confirming a renewal clause the system already surfaced is judgment.

The principle we kept returning to. Extraction is not the deliverable. A field a human can trust is the deliverable. Every design choice — what to extract, what to flag, what to leave to a person — gets made against that bar, not against a demo that looks impressive on a clean lease.

The gotchas the brochure never mentions

The demo works on the first lease you try. Then you point it at a real portfolio and the assumptions you didn't know you'd made start failing. Here are the ones that mattered.

Gotcha #1: the same field, written ten different ways. The single most underestimated problem in lease data is that humans never agreed on their own vocabulary. A field like the lease-versus-management form designation — whether an agreement is treated as a Lease or a Management Form — shows up with inconsistent values across the portfolio: different spellings, different casing, different conventions accreted over years and analysts. The expiration field is its own museum of typos (we have leases where the field is literally labelled LSExprition). If you trust the raw string, you get ten "categories" that are really one. We normalize to a known vocabulary on the way in, and we treat an unrecognized value as a thing to flag for a human — never as a new category to silently invent.

Gotcha #2: LeaseID is not unique. You would assume a field called LeaseID identifies one lease. It does not. In this data, LeaseIDs are non-unique — the same identifier can attach to more than one row. Any automation that joins, dedupes, or "looks up the lease" on LeaseID alone will quietly merge records that should stay separate, or answer a question about the wrong row. The fix is to stop pretending one column is a key and instead identify a row by the combination that's actually unique in practice — pairing EntityID with LeaseID — so a record is pinned to exactly one row of the report.

Gotcha #3: dates that aren't dates. Lease date fields arrive as a mix of real dates, near-dates, blanks, and free text where a date should be. A commencement that's "TBD," an expiration that's a typo, an option date expressed as a relative term rather than a calendar value — each one breaks naive date math and quietly poisons any renewal calendar built on top of it. We parse defensively, surface the unparseable rather than guessing, and never let a malformed date masquerade as a confident one.

The data doesn't lie to you loudly. It lies quietly — a non-unique key here, a typo'd category there — and a system that trusts it inherits every one of those lies.

Layer two: a Claude assistant that knows the data

Once the lease report exists, the natural next question from the people who use it is the one every analyst eventually asks a spreadsheet out loud: "which of these are expiring next year, and what's the rent?" So we put a data-aware Claude assistant directly on the lease report — a conversation layer that answers questions about the actual rows.

The word that matters is data-aware. A chat box that just sounds fluent about leases is worse than useless on a public company's numbers — a confident, wrong answer about a rent schedule is a liability, not a feature. The assistant earns trust by being structurally incapable of answering from vibes. It answers from the data or it doesn't answer.

The get_data tool. We gave the model a single, disciplined capability: a get_data tool. When a user asks something that requires facts, the model doesn't reach into its own memory — it calls get_data to pull the relevant rows from the live lease report, then reasons over what came back. The grounding is the whole architecture: the conversation is fluent, but the facts are fetched. If the data can't answer the question, the honest "I don't have that" beats a fluent fabrication every time. This is ordinary, well-bounded tool use — give the model exactly one way to touch reality, and make that the only way it's allowed to.

Row pinning by EntityID + LeaseID. Conversations drift. A user opens a specific lease, then asks three follow-ups that all mean "this one" without naming it. For the assistant to stay anchored, it has to know which row "this one" is — and because LeaseID isn't unique, it can't pin on that alone. So the assistant pins the active record by the EntityID / LeaseID pair, the same combination that uniquely identifies a row in the report. Pin the row, and follow-up questions resolve against exactly the lease the user is looking at — not a same-named neighbor, not a hallucinated blend of several.

Why the two layers share the same key. The non-unique-LeaseID gotcha from extraction is the same problem the assistant has to solve to pin a conversation. One data truth, enforced in both layers: a lease is identified by EntityID + LeaseID, never by LeaseID alone — on the way in, and on the way out.

Build and run discipline

Anyone can wire a model to a PDF and a chat box. Keeping it correct in production on a public company's data is a different discipline — and it's the part that doesn't demo.

  • Normalize at the boundary. Inconsistent form values and typo'd labels get mapped to a known vocabulary on ingest, and anything unrecognized is flagged for a person — never silently turned into a new category.
  • Identify rows honestly. EntityID + LeaseID is the real key. Every join, dedupe, lookup, and conversational pin uses it, because LeaseID alone will betray you.
  • Parse dates defensively. Surface the unparseable instead of guessing. A renewal calendar is only as trustworthy as its worst date field.
  • Ground every answer. The assistant answers from get_data or it says it can't. No fabricated rents, no invented dates, no confident nonsense on numbers that roll up to the financials.
  • Keep the human in the loop where judgment lives. Extraction moves people from transcription to verification; it doesn't pretend the verification step is optional.

Make the model structurally incapable of answering from vibes. It answers from the data, or it tells you it can't.

What this is part of

The lease abstract pipeline and its assistant don't stand alone — they're one piece of the platform we run for Sabra: roughly 20 custom SharePoint applications and 240-plus automated production workflows, live since 2020. AR-aging dashboards, payment approvals, a wire-transfer dashboard, and this lease work all sit on the same governed foundation. AI earns its place there the same way every other component does — by being correct on real data, every day, not impressive once in a demo.

If you're abstracting leases, contracts, or any dense documents by hand — or you want a chat assistant over a report that won't make things up — we start the same way every time: extract into trustworthy fields, identify your records by a key that's actually unique, and ground every AI answer in the real data through a single disciplined tool.
